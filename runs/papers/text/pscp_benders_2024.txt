Abstract
In this paper, we investigate the probabilistic set covering problem (PSCP) in which the
right-hand side is a binary random vector and the covering constraint is required to be satisfied with a prespecified probability. We consider the case with a finite discrete distribution of
the random vector, which usually arises in the context of the sample average approximation
approach. We develop an effective Benders decomposition (BD) algorithm for solving largescale PSCPs, which enjoys two key advantages: (i) the number of variables in the underlying
Benders reformulation is independent of the scenario size; and (ii) the Benders cuts can be
separated by an efficient combinatorial algorithm. For the special case that the random
vector is a combination of several independent random blocks/subvectors, we explicitly take
this kind of block structure into consideration and develop a more efficient BD algorithm.
Moreover, to further speed up the two proposed BD algorithms, we develop a class of strong
valid inequalities, which are guaranteed to be facet-defining for the polytope induced by the
probabilistic constraint. Numerical results on instances with up to one million scenarios
demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed BD algorithms over a black-box mixed integer
programming solver’s branch-and-cut and automatic BD algorithms and a state-of-the-art
algorithm in the literature.
Keywords: Benders decomposition · Large-scale optimization · Probabilistic set covering ·
Sample average approximations

(PSCP) falls in the class of probabilistic programs (also known as chance-constrained programs), which has been extensively investigated in the literature. We refer to the surveys Ahmed
and Shapiro (2008); Küçükyavuz and Sen (2017); Küçükyavuz and Jiang (2022); Prékopa (2003)
for a detailed discussion of probabilistic programs and the various algorithms. Beraldi and
Ruszczyński (2002) first investigated (PSCP) and developed a specialized branch-and-bound algorithm, which involves enumerating the so-called (1 − ϵ)-efficient points introduced by Prékopa
(1990) and solving a deterministic SCP for each (1−ϵ)-efficient point. Saxena et al. (2010) studied
the case with the assumption that the random vector ξ can be decomposed into T blocks/subvectors ξ 1 , . . . , ξ T formed by subsets Mt ⊆ [m], and ξ t1 and ξ t2 are independent for any two distinct

[τ ] = ∅ if τ = 0). Note that this assumption is not restrictive as it encompasses the cases of all
independent and dependent random variables (Beraldi and Ruszczyński, 2002). Problem (PSCP)
in this special case can be rewritten as

One weakness of the above two approaches for solving (PSCP) is that they require an enumeration of the (1 − ϵ)-efficient (and -inefficient) points of the distribution of ξ or ξ t , which could
be computationally demanding, especially when the dimensions are large. In addition, the MIP
formulation of Saxena et al. (2010) may grow exponentially with the dimensions of ξ t , making it
only capable of solving (PSCP) with small dimensions of ξ t . Luedtke and Ahmed (2008) addressed
this difficulty by using the sample average approximation (SAA) approach (Luedtke and Ahmed,
2008; Pagnoncelli et al., 2009), where (PSCP) is replaced by an approximation problem based
on Monte Carlo samples of the random vector ξ. The approximation problem is also a form of
(PSCP) but has a finite discrete distribution of ξ. As such, it can be reformulated as a deterministic MIP problem and solved by the state-of-the-art MIP solvers. In general, the selection of the
scenario size is crucial for the approximation quality; the larger the scenario size, the smaller the
approximation error; see Ahmed and Shapiro (2008); Luedtke and Ahmed (2008); Nemirovski and
Shapiro (2006) for related discussions. However, since the problem size of the MIP formulation
grows linearly with the number of scenarios, this also poses a new challenge for MIP solvers to
solve (PSCP) with a huge number of scenarios.

Contributions and outlines

The main motivation of this paper is to investigate decomposition based algorithms to solve largescale PSCPs. In particular,
(i) For PSCPs without the consideration of a block structure of ξ, i.e., problem (PSCP), we
investigate the MIP formulation of Luedtke and Ahmed (2008) and propose a customized
single-cut Benders decomposition (BD) algorithm where the scenario variables are projected
out from the problem formulation and the Benders cuts are separated on the fly. Two key
advantages of the proposed BD algorithm, which make it capable of solving (PSCP) with
a huge number of scenarios, are: (i) the number of variables in the underlying Benders
reformulation is equal to m + n but is independent of the number of scenarios of the random
data; and (ii) the Benders cuts can be separated by an efficient combinatorial algorithm.
(ii) For PSCPs with a block structure of ξ (Saxena et al., 2010), i.e., problem (PSCP’), we
develop a mixed integer nonlinear programming (MINLP) formulation, and propose a multicut customized BD algorithm for it. The proposed multi-cut BD algorithm explicitly takes
the block structure of the random vector ξ into account, and adds up to T Benders cuts
in a single iteration, which renders the BD algorithm able to converge more quickly. With
this feature, we show that although both MIP and MINLP formulations can be seen as
approximations of the PSCP in the context of the SAA approach, the multi-cut BD algorithm
for the MINLP formulation is even more efficient than the single-cut BD algorithm for the
MIP formulation.
(iii) To further speed up the two proposed BD algorithms, we leverage the sequential lifting
technique (Wolsey, 1976; Richard, 2011) and develop a class of strong valid inequalities—
lifted cover inequalities, which, unlike the Benders cuts, are guaranteed to be facet-defining
for the polytope induced by the probabilistic constraint. Moreover, to alleviate the potential
computational burden spent in the lifting procedure, we investigate a subclass of them—the
maximal clique inequalities, which enjoy light-weight algorithms for their computation and
separation. Equipped with the maximal clique inequalities, the two proposed BD algorithms
converge much faster, especially for instances with a small confidence parameter ϵ.
By extensive computational experiments on a testset of 7200 PSCP instances with up to 106
scenarios, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed BD algorithms for solving large-scale
PSCPs over existing state-of-the-art approaches. In particular, for PSCPs without the consideration of a block structure of the random vector ξ, the proposed BD algorithm is much more efficient
than a state-of-the-art MIP solver’s B&C and automatic BD algorithms (when the number of scenarios is large); for PSCPs with a block structure of ξ, the proposed BD algorithm is much more
robust than the state-of-the-art approach in Saxena et al. (2010) in terms of solving (PSCP’) with
different input parameters.
It deserves to mention that although the BD algorithms are proposed to tackle PSCPs, they can
also be applied to solve other variants of PSCPs that involve the probabilistic covering constraint
P{Ax ≥ ξ} ≥ 1 − ϵ where ξ is a binary random vector. In Online Appendix A, we consider three
variants of the PSCP investigated in Saxena et al. (2010) (i.e., the probabilistic versions of the
single-source capacitated facility location problem, capacitated warehouse location problem, and
capacitated p-median problem), and demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed BD algorithms
over existing state-of-the-art approaches.

Luedtke (2014) and Liu et al. (2016) developed decomposition algorithms along with the mixing inequalities (Atamtürk et al., 2000; Günlük and Pochet, 2001; Luedtke et al., 2010) for a more
general setting—the two-stage chance-constrained programs—in which the left-hand side of the
constraints may also involve uncertainty. The decomposition algorithms and mixing inequalities,
however, are fundamentally different from the ones proposed in this paper. First, the decomposition algorithms of Luedtke (2014) and Liu et al. (2016) attempt to project the second stage
variables out from the problem formulation but still involve scenario variables. In contrast, the
proposed BD algorithms exploit the structural properties of the PSCP and project the scenario
variables out from the problem formulations, making them capable of solving PSCPs with a huge
number of scenarios. Second, the mixing inequalities of Luedtke (2014) and Liu et al. (2016) are
generated from the so-called mixing set and involve the scenario variables. However, the proposed
lifted cover inequalities are derived from the polytope induced by the probabilistic constraint and
do not involve the scenario variables, rendering them able to be embedded in the proposed BD
algorithms.
An extended abstract of this paper can be found in 2024 INFORMS Optimization Society
(IOS) conference (Chen et al., 2024). Compared to its conference version, this paper investigates
the polyhedral structures of the 0-1 sets arising in PSCPs with and without a block structure of
ξ, and develops strong valid inequalities to improve the computational performance of the BD
algorithms for solving PSCPs. Moreover, extensive computational results are also reported in this
paper.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the mathematical formulations for (PSCP) and (PSCP’), and provides a favorable property for the formulations. Section 3
develops the BD algorithms for solving the two formulations. Section 4 derives the strong valid inequalities to improve the performance of the BD algorithms. Section 5 presents the computational
results. Finally, Section 6 draws the conclusion.

by removing the integrality constraints z ∈ {0, 1}s (respectively, z ∈ {0, 1}s×T ) and replacing the
integrality constraints v ∈ {0, 1}m by v ≤ 1. The following proposition shows that this operation
does not change the optimal values of problems (MIP) and (MINLP).
Proposition 2.2. Problems (MIP) and (MINLP) are equivalent to (MIP’) and (MINLP’), respectively, in terms of sharing the same optimal values.
Proof. Proof Let oMIP and oMIP’ be the optimal values of problems (MIP) and (MIP’). Clearly,
oMIP’ ≤ oMIP . To prove the other direction, suppose that (x, v, z) is an optimal solution of (MIP’).
Let v̄ and z̄ be defined by v̄ = min{Ax, 1} ∈ {0, 1}m and z̄i = mink∈Mi v̄k ∈ {0, 1}, where
Mi = {k ∈ [m] : i ∈ Sk }, i ∈ [s]. By (1), it follows v ≤ min{Ax, 1} = v̄, and by (2), it
follows zi ≤ mink∈Mi vk ≤ mink∈Mi v̄k = z̄i . Thus (x, v̄, z̄) must be a feasible solution of problem
(MIP). As a result, oMIP = c⊤ x ≤ oMIP’ . This shows that oMIP = oMIP’ and the equivalence of
problems (MIP) and (MIP’). The proof for the equivalence of problems (MINLP) and (MINLP’)
is analogous.
Although the integrality constraints v ∈ {0, 1}m can be relaxed into v ≤ 1, Luedtke and Ahmed
(2008) noted that leaving v ∈ {0, 1}m in problem (MIP) enables B&C solvers to favor branching
on variables v and effectively improves the overall performance. Our preliminary experiments
also justified this statement (for both (MIP’) and (MINLP’)). Due to this, we decide to leave the
integrality constraints v ∈ {0, 1}m in the two formulations.

4.4

Clique-based inequalities

As shown in Section 4.1, in order to obtain a lifted cover inequality (40) from a minimal cover
inequality (35), one has to solve m−|C| lifting problems of the form (38), which could be computationally demanding (even though we can solve the LP relaxations to obtain valid lifting coefficients
when ξ has a finite discrete distribution with or without a block structure). To further speed up
the computational procedure, in this section, we will present a subclass of the lifted cover inequalities, which can be constructed without the need for the potentially computationally expensive
lifting procedure, making them particularly suitable to be embedded in a B&C framework to solve
the PSCP.
To proceed, we consider a minimal cover C with size |C| = 2, which we call two-cover. The
number of two-covers is upper bounded by O(m2 ). Therefore, by checking whether λ({j, k}) > ϵ
holds for all pairs j, k ∈ [m] with j ̸= k (where λ(·) is defined in (34)), we can identify all two-covers
and construct the two-cover induced graph as follows.
Definition 4.3. A two-cover induced graph G = ([m], E) contains the edge {j, k} if and only if
λ({j, k}) > ϵ.
Letting C = {j, k}, then the cover inequality (35) reduces to vj +vk ≥ 1. Two-cover inequalities
can be strengthened using the concept of cliques. More specifically, a clique with vertex set Q is
a fully connected subgraph in G; that is, for any pair j ∈ Q and k ∈ Q\{j}, it follows {j, k} ∈ E.
A maximal clique is a clique in graph G that cannot be extended by including any more vertices.
For a maximal clique with vertex set Q, since at most one variable vk , k ∈ Q, can take the value
of zero, we obtain the maximal clique inequality
X
vk ≥ |Q| − 1,
(48)
k∈Q

which is valid for X and stronger than the two-cover inequalities vj + vk ≥ 1, j, k ∈ Q.
For any maximal clique with vertex set Q, the two-cover inequality vj + vk ≥ 1 with j, k ∈ Q
is valid for X ({j, k}). By first lifting variables {vk′ : k ′ ∈ Q\{j, k}} (with any lifting order of
Q\{j, k}) and then lifting variables {vk′ : k ′ ∈ [m]\Q} (with any lifting order of [m]\Q), we
can also obtain the maximal clique inequality (48). As a result, the maximal clique inequalities
are special cases of the lifted cover inequalities in (40). Therefore, it immediately follows from
Lemma 4.2 that
Corollary 4.4. The maximal clique inequalities are facet-defining for conv(X ).
Although the maximal clique inequalities are special cases of lifted cover inequalities, they are
very attractive from a computational perspective due to the following reasons. First, compared
with the lifted cover inequalities whose computation requires to solve m − |C| lifting problems of
the form (38) (or their LP relaxations), the maximal clique inequalities admit a much more efficient computational procedure—one just needs to extend a clique in a graph until no more vertices
can be included with the complexity of O(m2 ). Second, given a solution v ∗ ∈ [0, 1]m encountered
15

in the B&C algorithm (of solving the Benders reformulation of problems (MIP) and (MINLP)),
the problem of finding a violated maximal clique inequality is equivalent to solving a maximum
weighted clique problem (Atamtürk et al., 2000; Achterberg, 2007). Although this problem is
NP-hard (Grötschel et al., 1993, Theorem 9.2.9), there are various effective heuristic algorithms
for finding violated inequalities in the literature; see Atamtürk et al. (2000); Achterberg (2007);
Burke et al. (2012); Rebennack et al. (2012); Brito and Santos (2021) among many of them. Moreover, state-of-the-art MIP solvers have implemented effective algorithms to find violated maximal
clique inequalities, which allows us to simply state the two-cover inequalities as constraints and
then facilitate the powerful MIP solvers’ internal separation procedure for finding violated clique
inequalities.

5

Computational results

In this section, we present computational results to illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed
BD algorithms for solving PSCPs based on formulations (MIP) and (MINLP). In particular, we
first evaluate the performance effect of the proposed lifted cover inequalities for the proposed BD
algorithm. Then, we present computational results to show the effectiveness of the proposed BD
algorithms over state-of-the-art approaches for solving problems (MIP) and (MINLP). Finally, we
compare the performance of the proposed BD algorithms for solving problems (MIP) and (MINLP)
when applying them as sample approximation problems to solve PSCPs. In Online Appendix C,
we further present the computational results to analyze the effect of the scenario size on the quality
of the solution returned by solving the sample approximation problem.

5.1

Implementation

The proposed BD algorithms were implemented in Julia 1.7.3 using CPLEX 20.1.0. We use a
branch-and-Benders-cut (B&BC) approach (Rahmaniani et al., 2017) to implement the proposed
BD algorithms for solving the PSCPs, where the Benders feasibility cuts are separated on the
fly at the nodes of the search tree. In addition, when using the lifted cover inequalities, we
separate them at the root node of the search tree. This approach can be implemented via the cut
callback framework available in modern general-purpose MIP solvers and has been widely applied
to implement the BD algorithm for various problems; see Gendron et al. (2016); Fischetti et al.
(2016); Bodur and Luedtke (2017); Cordeau et al. (2019) among many of them. Before starting the
B&BC algorithm, we apply the preprocessing technique investigated in Lejeune (2008); Lejeune
and Noyan (2010); Luedtke and Ahmed (2008) to simplify formulations (MIP) and (MINLP),
and add all two-cover inequalities in Section 4.4 as constraints to initialize the relaxed master
problem. Note that with the two-cover inequalities in the relaxed master problem, CPLEX can
automatically strengthen the two-cover inequalities into a subset of maximal clique inequalities
(48) and separate the remaining maximal clique inequalities on the fly.
To further speed up the B&BC algorithm, we follow Fischetti et al. (2016) to implement a
restart mechanism to increase the information available (i.e., effective cuts and heuristic solutions)
at the root node, so that more variable fixings and generation of internal cuts will be performed
at the root node. Specifically, before entering into the final B&C process, we terminate the
algorithm immediately after solving the root node (through setting CPX PARAM NODELIM=1), state
the generated Benders feasibility cuts and lifted cover inequalities, stored in our own data structure,
as static constraints in the relaxed master problem, and update the incumbent solution. We repeat
16

the above process twice and then go to the final B&C process.
In our experiments, the parameters of CPLEX were set to run the code in a single-threaded
mode, with a time limit of 7200 seconds and a relative MIP gap of 0%. Unless otherwise stated,
all other parameters in CPLEX were set to their default values. The computational experiments

6

Conclusions and future work

In this paper, we have considered the PSCPs with an arbitrary finite discrete distribution of ξ and
with a block structure of ξ. We developed efficient BD algorithms for solving the MIP and MINLP
formulations of the PSCPs where the scenario variables are projected out from the formulation
and the Benders feasibility cuts are separated on the fly. Three key features of the proposed
BD algorithms, which make them particularly suitable for solving large-scale PSCPs, are: (i) the
numbers of variables in the underlying Benders reformulations are independent of the scenario sizes
of ξ; (ii) the Benders feasibility cuts can be separated by an efficient polynomial-time algorithm;
and (iii) they are equipped with a class of strong valid inequalities—lifted cover inequalities,
which renders them able to converge more quickly. By extensive computational experiments, we
demonstrated the effectiveness of our proposed BD algorithms for solving large-scale PSCPs. In
particular, for PSCPs with an arbitrary finite discrete distribution of ξ, the proposed BD algorithm
is much more efficient than the B&C and automatic BD algorithms of CPLEX (when the number
of scenarios is large); for PSCPs with a block structure of ξ, the proposed BD algorithm is much
more robust than the state-of-the-art approach in Saxena et al. (2010) in terms of solving problems
with different input parameters.
As observed in Section 5.3, the proposed approach for computing the general lifted cover
inequalities in Sections 4.2 and 4.3 is still computationally demanding, making it generally not
beneficial to the proposed BD algorithms for solving PSCPs. As for future work, we shall develop
acceleration strategies for efficiently solving the lifting problems. One interesting direction is to
analyze the relations among the lifting problems and design warm-start algorithms based on the
solutions of previously solved lifting problems, as to speed up the computation of the lifted cover
inequalities. In addition, it would be interesting to extend the proposed BD approach to other
variants of the PSCP, such as the robust version (Lutter et al., 2017; Shen and Jiang, 2023) and
the conditional value-at-risk based risk-averse version (Deng et al., 2018; Wu and Küçükyavuz,
2020) of the SCP.
